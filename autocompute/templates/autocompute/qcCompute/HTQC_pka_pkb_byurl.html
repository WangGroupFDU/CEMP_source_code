    1. Introduction to the High-Throughput pkb (pka) Calculation Module

    This module facilitates high-throughput quantum chemistry calculations based on Gaussian16, incorporating both structure optimization and single-point energy calculations.

  • Structure Optimization:
  • Theoretical level: B3LYP/6-311G**
  • Dispersion correction: GD3BJ
  • Frequency correction factor: 0.9682
  • Implicit solvent model: SMD (water)
  • The program automatically checks for optimization failures or imaginary frequencies and performs error correction if necessary.
  • Single-Point Energy Calculation:
  • Theoretical level: M062X/6-311+(2d,p)
  • Dispersion correction: GD3
  • Implicit solvent model: SMD (water)
  • The program automatically checks for calculation failures and performs error correction if necessary.
    • Users need to download the provided Excel file, fill in the calculation parameters, and upload the completed file. The program will automatically execute the calculations and download the results upon completion.

    • Calculation Methods for pKa and pKb

    The acid dissociation constant (Ka) and base dissociation constant (Kb) are fundamental parameters in understanding the strength of acids and bases. They are commonly expressed in their logarithmic forms, pKa and pKb, respectively. This document outlines detailed methods for calculating these constants using thermodynamic data and demonstrates the mathematical relationships involved.

    Relationship between pKa and pKb

    At 25°C, in aqueous solutions, the relationship between pKa and pKb for a conjugate acid-base pair is given by:

    $$ pK_a + pK_b = 14 $$

    This equation allows the calculation of one constant if the other is known.

    Calculating pKa from Gibbs Free Energy

    The dissociation of an acid (HA) in water can be represented as:

    $$ \text{HA} \leftrightarrow \text{H}^+ + \text{A}^- $$

    The standard Gibbs free energy change (ΔG°) for this reaction is calculated using the standard Gibbs free energies of formation:

    $$ \Delta G^\circ = G^\circ_{\text{A}^-} + G^\circ_{\text{H}^+} - G^\circ_{\text{HA}} $$

    The acid dissociation constant (Ka) is related to the Gibbs free energy change by:

    $$ \Delta G^\circ = -RT \ln K_a $$

    Solving for pKa:

    $$ pK_a = \frac{\Delta G^\circ}{2.303RT} $$

    Where:

    • R = 8.314 J·mol−1·K−1 (universal gas constant)
    • T = Temperature in Kelvin (usually 298 K)
    • 2.303 converts natural logarithm to base 10 logarithm

    Calculating pKb from Gibbs Free Energy

    For a base (B), the protonation reaction in water is:

    $$ \text{B} + \text{H}^+ \leftrightarrow \text{BH}^+ $$

    The standard Gibbs free energy change is:

    $$ \Delta G^\circ = G^\circ_{\text{BH}^+} - G^\circ_{\text{B}} - G^\circ_{\text{H}^+} $$

    The base dissociation constant (Kb) relates to its conjugate acid's pKa by:

    $$ pK_b = 14 - pK_a $$

    Alternatively, you can calculate Kb directly using the Gibbs free energy change for the base hydrolysis reaction.

    Practical Calculation Steps

    1. Compute Gibbs Free Energies: Use quantum chemistry calculations (e.g., DFT) to obtain the standard Gibbs free energies (G°) of the species involved, typically in Hartrees.
    2. Convert Units: Convert energies from Hartrees to kJ/mol or kcal/mol using the conversion factors:
      • 1 Hartree = 2625.5 kJ/mol
      • 1 Hartree = 627.51 kcal/mol
    3. Include the Proton's Free Energy: The standard Gibbs free energy of the proton in aqueous solution is typically taken as:
      $$ G^\circ_{\text{H}^+} = -272.18\ \text{kcal/mol} $$
    4. Calculate ΔG°: Apply the appropriate formula for ΔG° based on the reaction.
    5. Compute pKa or pKb: Use the relationship between ΔG° and pK to calculate the desired constant.

    Conclusion

    The calculation of pKa and pKb involves thermodynamic principles and requires careful consideration of the Gibbs free energies of the species involved. Accurate calculations must account for solvation effects, temperature, and entropy contributions. Computational chemistry methods can provide estimates, but experimental validation is essential for precise values.

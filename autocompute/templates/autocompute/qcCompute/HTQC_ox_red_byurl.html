    1. Introduction to the High-Throughput redox potential Calculation Module

    This module facilitates high-throughput quantum chemistry calculations based on Gaussian16 or ORCA, incorporating both structure optimization and single-point energy calculations. Users can choose between Gaussian16 and ORCA for their calculations.

    Calculation Software Options:

    • Gaussian16: A commercial software requiring a license (available only to collaborating users). The calculation results on this platform are intended for educational purposes only and cannot be used for publication or commercial purposes without purchasing a license.
    • ORCA: Free for academic users. Suitable for both main group elements and transition metal-related calculations.

    Important Note on Software Usage:

    • Calculation results from Gaussian16 and ORCA cannot be mixed. Users must choose one software for all calculations in a project.

    Structure Optimization:

    • For Gaussian16:
      • Theoretical level: B3LYP/6-311G**
      • Dispersion correction: GD3BJ
      • Frequency correction factor: 0.9682
    • For ORCA:
      • Theoretical level: B3LYP/def2-TZVP
      • Acceleration method: RIJCOSX
      • Dispersion correction: GD3BJ

    The program automatically checks for optimization failures or imaginary frequencies and performs error correction if necessary.

    Single-Point Energy Calculation:

    • For Gaussian16:
      • Theoretical level: M062X/6-311+(2d,p)
      • Dispersion correction: GD3
    • For ORCA:
      • Theoretical level: wB97M-V/def2-TZVP
      • Acceleration method: RIJCOSX

    The program automatically checks for calculation failures and performs error correction if necessary.

    Users need to download the provided Excel file, fill in the calculation parameters, and upload the completed file. The program will automatically execute the calculations and download the results upon completion.

    Calculation of Molecular Oxidation and Reduction Potentials

    Introduction

    The oxidation and reduction potentials of molecules are fundamental properties in electrochemistry, crucial for understanding redox reactions, designing electrochemical cells, and exploring electron transfer processes. Computational chemistry provides valuable tools to calculate these potentials using quantum chemical methods.

    Theoretical Background

    The oxidation potential (Eox) and reduction potential (Ered) can be calculated using the Gibbs free energy changes associated with the oxidation and reduction reactions, respectively. According to thermodynamics, the relationship between the Gibbs free energy change (ΔG) and the electrode potential (E) is given by:

    $$ E = -\frac{\Delta G}{nF} $$

    Where:

    • n is the number of electrons transferred (usually 1 for single-electron processes).
    • F is the Faraday constant (approximately 96485 C·mol−1).

    Calculating Gibbs Free Energy Changes

    The Gibbs free energy change for a redox reaction can be calculated using the total Gibbs free energies of the reactants and products obtained from quantum chemical calculations:

    $$ \Delta G = G_{\text{products}} - G_{\text{reactants}} $$

    For oxidation (loss of an electron):

    $$ \text{M} \rightarrow \text{M}^+ + e^- $$ $$ \Delta G_{\text{ox}} = G_{\text{M}^+} - G_{\text{M}} $$

    For reduction (gain of an electron):

    $$ \text{M} + e^- \rightarrow \text{M}^- $$ $$ \Delta G_{\text{red}} = G_{\text{M}^-} - G_{\text{M}} $$

    Electrode Potential Calculation

    Combining the equations, the electrode potential relative to the standard hydrogen electrode (SHE) can be calculated as:

    $$ E = -\frac{\Delta G}{nF} - E_{\text{SHE}} $$

    Where ESHE is the absolute potential of the standard hydrogen electrode, approximately 4.44 V.

    Computational Procedure

    1. Geometry Optimization: Optimize the molecular geometries of the neutral molecule, its cation (for oxidation), and its anion (for reduction) using a suitable level of theory (e.g., DFT with B3LYP functional and appropriate basis set).
    2. Frequency Calculation: Perform frequency calculations to obtain thermal corrections and ensure that the optimized structures are at energy minima (no imaginary frequencies).
    3. Single Point Energy Calculation: Calculate the total electronic energies and thermal corrections to Gibbs free energies for each species.
    4. Compute Gibbs Free Energies: Obtain the total Gibbs free energy for each species:
      $$ G_{\text{total}} = E_{\text{electronic}} + G_{\text{thermal}} $$
    5. Calculate ΔG: Compute the Gibbs free energy changes for oxidation and reduction:
      $$ \Delta G_{\text{ox}} = G_{\text{M}^+} - G_{\text{M}} $$ $$ \Delta G_{\text{red}} = G_{\text{M}^-} - G_{\text{M}} $$
    6. Calculate Electrode Potentials: Use the Nernst equation to calculate the electrode potentials relative to SHE:
      $$ E_{\text{ox}} = -\frac{\Delta G_{\text{ox}}}{nF} - E_{\text{SHE}} $$ $$ E_{\text{red}} = -\frac{\Delta G_{\text{red}}}{nF} - E_{\text{SHE}} $$
    7. Convert Units: Since computational energies are in Hartree and potentials are in volts, convert the energy units:
      $$ 1 \text{ Hartree} = 27.2114 \text{ eV} $$ $$ \Delta G (\text{eV}) = \Delta G (\text{Hartree}) \times 27.2114 $$
    8. Final Calculation: The electrode potentials can be simplified as:
      $$ E = \frac{\Delta G (\text{eV})}{n} - E_{\text{SHE}} $$

    Notes and Considerations

    Number of Electrons: Ensure that the correct number of electrons (n) is used in calculations; for single-electron processes, n = 1.

    Reference Electrode: Be consistent with the reference electrode potential; the absolute potential of SHE is approximately 4.44 V but can vary slightly in literature.

    Conclusion

    Calculating the oxidation and reduction potentials of molecules using quantum chemical methods involves careful consideration of computational details and thermodynamic principles. Accurate results depend on the level of theory, inclusion of solvent effects, and proper unit conversions.
